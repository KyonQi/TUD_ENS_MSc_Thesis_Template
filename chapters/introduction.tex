\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chp:introduction}
The Internet of Things (IoT) has made microcontrollers ubiquitous in our daily lives. Owing to their low cost, energy efficiency, and compact form factor, MCUs are widely employed in various applications, such as cameras, wearable devices, industrial sensors, robots, drones, and environmental monitoring systems, where they serve as the primary computing platform for sensing and control. Recently, deploying compressed deep learning models on these devices, a paradigm known as Tiny Machine Learning (TinyML), has gained significant attention [\textcolor{red}{refs}]. Executing a neural network on the local device preserves data privacy, removes cloud dependency, and guarantees low and predictable latency that sensing and control applications require. 

However, the constrained resources of MCUs, including limited memory, storage, and processing power, pose significant challenges for deploying capable deep learning models on MCUs. A typical edge device offers hundreds of kilobytes to about one megabyte of Static Random-Access Memory (SRAM). While the model weights could reside in Flash memory, the intermediate feature maps (i.e., activations) generated during inference must fit in SRAM. For many Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), the peak activation footprint rather than the model size becomes the limiting factor for deployment on MCUs. When the activation exceeds the SRAM of the target device, the model simply cannot be deployed, no matter how small its weights have been compressed.

The activation constraint is a critical bottleneck for deploying efficient CNN models built from Inverted Residual Blocks (IRBs) on MCUs, such as MobileNetV2, MCUNet, MnasNet, and ProxylessNAS [\textcolor{red}{ref}].  An IRB usually first expands the channel dimension by an expansion ratio, then applies a depthwise convolution, and finally projects the feature maps back to a compact output channel dimension. It is compute-efficient, but it creates a large \emph{temporary} activation that exists only inside the block. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tbd}, these temporary activations between the expansion and projection layers can be significantly larger than the limited SRAM budget of MCUs, creating highly unbalanced activation footprints across layers. Typically, the peak activation occurs temporarily only inside the IRB, after channel expansion and before channel projection. 

Existing TinyML researches, including quantization, pruning, and neural architecture search, and memory-aware runtime, have made significant progress in single MCU deployment [\textcolor{red}{refs}]. However, when the acitvation footprint exceeds the SRAM capacity of single MCU, developers still need to redesign the model architecture, reduce the input resolution, use repeated patch-based inference, or move the task to a more powerful device.   

Many practical deployments already contain several connected edge devices, such as multi-camera environments, distributed industrial sensing arrays, and robot or drone swarms. By distributing the execution of a single network across these devices, their memory and computation can be aggregated, enabling larger models or higher input resolutions under the same per-device SRAM limit. This thesis studies how to make such collaborative execution practical and efficient.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:intro:problem-statement}
Split and collaborative inference has been studied for cloud and edge systems, embedded clusters, and, more recently, networked MCUs~\cite{kang2017neurosurgeon,zhao2018deepthings,lu2026splitcnn}. These systems demonstrate that distributing a neural network can relieve per-device memory pressure and pool computation. However, they mainly optimise \emph{where} and \emph{how} to split the model, organising execution around layer-wise splits, sequential model partitions, or fine-grained sub-layer tasks. What they do not address is the communication boundary induced by the block structure of modern TinyML CNNs. When the distributed runtime communicates at layer or sub-layer boundaries, the temporarily expanded activation of an IRB becomes a network-level object. It must be transmitted through the network, buffered at the coordinating device, and redistributed for the next layer. The consequence is a dual bottleneck that is specific to MCU-class clusters. The inter-device links carry traffic dominated by tensors that were never meant to leave a device, and the coordinator must hold intermediate activations that are larger than any single MCU could absorb.

This thesis therefore asks the following research question. \emph{How can a standard TinyML CNN be transformed into a communication-efficient distributed execution across a group of networked MCUs?} Answering it raises two challenges.

\paragraph{Challenge 1. Generating a distributed execution plan from a standard model.}
A single-device model file does not contain the information distributed execution needs. A networked MCU runtime must know, for each worker, which spatial partition it owns, which weight slices it needs, which quantisation scales and zero-points apply, which layers should be grouped into one execution unit, which activations must be cached for residual connections, and which boundary rows its neighbours require. These decisions further depend on the number of workers and on the SRAM budget of each device. Without an automatic preparation pipeline, distributed TinyML deployment degenerates into model-specific and hardware-specific manual work that is hard to reproduce, to port to a new CNN, or to scale to a different cluster size.

\paragraph{Challenge 2. Making the distributed execution communication- and memory-efficient.}
Even with a valid distributed representation, a naive layer-wise schedule can be highly inefficient, since every expanded activation crosses the network twice and resides in full at the coordinator. The key design problem is therefore not simply how to split computation across workers, but how to choose execution boundaries that avoid exposing large temporary activations to the network. On networked MCUs, a small and bounded amount of local recomputation can be far preferable to repeatedly transmitting expanded feature maps through the coordinator, and a practical framework must reason about this trade-off explicitly.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro-contributions}
This thesis answers the research question with a deployment framework organised in two stages. An offline preprocessing stage quantises the model and compiles it into a communication-aware execution plan and a set of per-worker manifests. An online stage then executes the plan on a coordinator-worker cluster, where a coordinator schedules tasks and assembles outputs while the MCU workers execute their assigned spatial partitions. On top of this runtime the framework applies two jointly designed mechanisms. \emph{Block-wise local execution} uses the inverted residual block rather than the layer as the communication unit, so that each expanded activation is materialised and consumed inside a worker and never crosses the network. \emph{Cross-block data reuse} lets workers retain the rows they produced for one block and exchange only the thin boundary regions the next block requires, subject to correctness conditions that the framework analyses offline. We prototype the framework on a cluster of up to six Teensy 4.1 boards and evaluate it on four representative IRB-based CNNs.

In summary, this thesis makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We identify a framework gap in collaborative TinyML inference. Existing model formats and runtimes do not expose the partition, boundary, residual, and memory information that communication-efficient execution of CNNs across networked MCUs requires.
  \item We design an offline preprocessing pipeline that converts a quantised CNN into per-worker manifests, encoding spatial partitions, weight placement, quantisation metadata, block grouping, residual dependencies, and boundary reuse conditions, together with a manifest-driven coordinator-worker runtime that executes the plan without making global decisions at run time.
  \item We propose two communication-aware mechanisms, block-wise local execution and cross-block data reuse, which keep expanded activations local to the workers and reduce redundant boundary transfers across consecutive blocks. We analyse the conditions under which reuse preserves correctness, and we develop an overlap-ratio cost model that characterises when block-wise execution stops paying off and enables an adaptive per-block choice.
  \item We implement the framework end to end on real hardware and validate that the distributed execution is bit-identical to the single-device quantised model, so the efficiency gains cost no accuracy.
  \item We evaluate the framework on a real MCU cluster with MobileNetV2, MCUNet, MnasNet, and ProxylessNAS. Compared with naive layer-wise execution on four workers, it reduces end-to-end latency by 30\% to 47\%, cuts early-block communication by more than 80\%, and lowers the coordinator peak memory from 735~KB to 158~KB on MobileNetV2-0.35.
\end{itemize}

\section{Thesis Outline}
\label{sec:intro-thesis-outline}
The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{chp:related-work} reviews single-device TinyML techniques and split and collaborative inference systems, and positions this work against them. Chapter~\ref{chp:system_design} presents the design of the framework, including the two-stage architecture, the offline planner, the online coordinator-worker runtime, the two optimisation mechanisms, and the cost model and correctness conditions that govern them. Chapter~\ref{chp:implementation} describes the realisation of the design on real hardware, covering the offline toolchain, the coordinator runtime, the wire protocol, the worker firmware on the Teensy 4.1, and the validation of numerical correctness. Chapter~\ref{chp:evaluation} evaluates the framework on a cluster of up to six MCUs across four CNNs, reporting communication overhead, end-to-end latency, peak memory, and an ablation of the two mechanisms. Chapter~\ref{chp:conclusions} concludes the thesis and discusses directions for future work.
